Power Factor Correction

Make the power supply look like a resistor.

Power factor correction is the front-end discipline of drawing useful AC-grid current: sinusoidal, low-harmonic, and aligned with the line voltage. The big idea is simple: the load should not demand giant current pulses or reactive circulation just to deliver real power.

Intuition

Real power is the part that becomes useful work or heat in the load. Apparent power is what the grid, wiring, breakers, and semiconductors must carry. Power factor is the ratio between the two:

\[ PF = \frac{P}{S} = \frac{\text{real power}}{\text{apparent power}} \]

A poor power factor means more RMS current for the same delivered watts. For a 60 W adapter on 115 VAC, ideal PF draws about 0.52 A RMS; at PF = 0.4, it draws about 1.3 A RMS for the same real power.

Why AC/DC inputs distort current

A simple diode bridge plus bulk capacitor does not draw current smoothly through the entire line cycle. The bridge conducts mostly when the rectified line voltage rises above the capacitor voltage plus diode drops. That creates narrow recharge pulses instead of a clean sine current.

Those current pulses contain harmonics. Total harmonic distortion describes how much current exists outside the fundamental line-frequency component:

\[ THD = \frac{\sqrt{\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} I_n^2}}{I_1} \]

With sinusoidal voltage, power factor is hurt by both phase displacement and harmonic distortion. A useful compact relation is:

\[ PF \approx \frac{\cos\phi}{\sqrt{1 + THD^2}} \]
Design intuition: displacement PF asks “is the current sine delayed?” Distortion PF asks “is the current even a sine?” PFC has to address both.

The PFC stage

A PFC converter is inserted between the rectifier and the downstream DC/DC converter. Passive PFC adds line-frequency inductance, which is simple but bulky and limited over universal input. Active PFC uses a full switching converter and control loop to shape input current over a wide line range.

The most common active PFC topology is the boost converter. One reason is physical: the boost inductor is on the input side, so the source sees inductor current instead of a hard-switched MOSFET current step.

Boost PFC power path

During MOSFET on-time, the inductor current rises with approximately \( V_{IN}/L \) while the output capacitor feeds the load. During MOSFET off-time, the inductor delivers energy through the boost diode to the output. Because boost only steps up, a universal single-phase PFC bus is usually regulated above the highest expected AC peak, commonly around the 380–400 V range.

Control modes: CrCM, CCM, and interleaving

ModeHow it controls currentBest intuitionTypical use
CrCM / TM Constant or modulated on-time; next cycle starts when inductor current reaches zero or a valley. Simple, valley-switched, no boost-diode reverse recovery, but very high ripple and high peak current. Often attractive below a few hundred watts.
CCM Fixed switching frequency with average current-mode control; duty cycle tracks a sinusoidal current reference. Lower ripple and peak current, better THD at higher power, but hard switching and diode recovery matter. Common above a few hundred watts into kW-class designs.
Interleaved PFC Two or more boost phases switch out of phase. Each phase still has ripple, but the input sees partial cancellation and lower per-phase RMS stress. Useful for higher power, lower profile, smaller EMI filter, and improved thermal spreading.
The practical design choice is not “which mode is best?” It is “which loss mechanism is hardest in this product: switching loss, conduction loss, EMI filtering, height, cost, or control complexity?”

Worked example: same watts, different PF

Problem. A 60 W adapter operates from 115 VAC. Compare RMS input current at PF = 1.0 and PF = 0.4.

\[ I_{RMS} = \frac{P}{V_{RMS} \cdot PF} \]

Ideal PF: \( I = 60/(115 \cdot 1.0) = 0.522\,A \).

Poor PF: \( I = 60/(115 \cdot 0.4) = 1.30\,A \).

Takeaway. The load receives the same 60 W, but the wiring and upstream power system must carry much more current when PF is poor.

EMI lens

The boost PFC stage is still a switching converter, so layout and parasitics matter. Differential-mode noise is tied to ripple current flowing line-to-neutral; common-mode noise is tied to high-dv/dt nodes capacitively coupling to earth or chassis. Interleaving can help by canceling input ripple, while bridgeless topologies can improve conduction loss but often make common-mode EMI and current sensing more challenging.
