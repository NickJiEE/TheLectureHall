LLC Resonant Converter

Use resonance to switch softly.

After the PFC stage creates a high-voltage DC bus, the downstream converter still has a hard job: isolate, step down, regulate, and stay efficient. The LLC resonant converter solves this by letting the tank current do useful timing work, enabling zero-voltage switching over a wide operating range.

Why resonant conversion?

Hard-switched PWM converters spend part of each transition with both voltage and current present in the switch. That loss limits switching frequency and power density. A resonant converter reshapes voltage and current waveforms so switching events happen under friendlier conditions.

The LLC tank is especially useful because it can provide ZVS turn-on for MOSFETs and naturally reduce reverse-recovery stress on the secondary rectifiers. That is why LLC converters show up so often after a PFC front end in adapters, server supplies, TV power boards, and telecom supplies.

The LLC tank

The tank contains a series resonant inductor \(L_r\), series resonant capacitor \(C_r\), and magnetizing inductance \(L_m\). The key difference from a simple series resonant converter is that \(L_m\) can act as both a passive load and a second resonant participant depending on the switching frequency, load, and interval within the switching cycle.

There are two important resonant frequencies:

\[ f_{r1} = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{L_r C_r}} \qquad f_{r2} = \frac{1}{2\pi\sqrt{(L_m + L_r) C_r}} \]

Designers often place the normal high-line operating point near \(f_{r1}\), where the tank gain is near unity and circulating energy is controlled. When input voltage drops, the controller reduces switching frequency to get more voltage gain.

Operating regions

RegionFrequency rangeWhat the tank is doingWhy it matters
Region 1 \( f_{sw} > f_{r1} \) Looks similar to a series resonant converter. \(L_m\) is clamped by reflected output voltage and acts like a passive load. ZVS can be maintained, and no-load regulation is easier than a pure SRC because \(L_m\) gives the tank a load path.
Region 2 \( f_{r2} < f_{sw} < f_{r1} \) First \(L_r\) resonates with \(C_r\); after secondary current falls to zero, \(L_m\) joins the resonance with \(L_r\) and \(C_r\). This is the special LLC region: gain can rise below \(f_{r1}\) while the tank remains inductive enough for ZVS.
Region 3 Very low \( f_{sw} \), below the useful ZVS range The tank behavior becomes capacitive / ZCS-like. Avoid it for MOSFET converters because turn-on can become lossy and stressful.

Region 2 sequence

Region 2 is the mode worth slowing down for. In one half-cycle, the transition can be understood as three intervals:

  1. Mode 1: one MOSFET has just turned off; resonant current is negative and flows through the opposite body diode, preparing ZVS before the gate is applied.
  2. Mode 2: the MOSFET conducts; the secondary rectifier conducts; \(L_m\) is clamped by reflected output voltage; \(L_r\) and \(C_r\) dominate the resonance.
  3. Mode 3: \(I_{Lr}\) meets \(I_{Lm}\), output current reaches zero, the secondary disconnects, and \(L_m\) joins \(L_r\) in resonance with \(C_r\).
The magnetizing current is not just a penalty. It is the tool that discharges switch capacitances and creates ZVS, even at very light load. But too much magnetizing current increases circulating RMS current, so \(L_m/L_r\) is a core design tradeoff.

Design flow

1. Choose the transformer ratio

At the nominal high-line bus, choose the turns ratio so the converter operates close to unity gain around \(f_{r1}\). For a half-bridge LLC:

\[ n \approx \frac{V_{in}}{2V_o} \]

2. Choose \(f_{r1}\)

Pick the nominal series resonance based on target switching frequency, magnetic size, device losses, and controller range. Higher frequency shrinks magnetics but increases AC winding loss and layout sensitivity.

3. Choose \(L_m/L_r\)

A smaller \(L_m\) helps narrow frequency range and support ZVS, but raises magnetizing current. A larger \(L_m\) reduces circulating current but may demand wider frequency range or reduce ZVS margin.

4. Check gain, current, and stress

Verify worst-case low-line gain, light-load behavior, resonant capacitor RMS current and voltage, switch turn-off current, rectifier stress, and transformer thermal limits.

Worked example: first turns-ratio estimate

Problem. Estimate the transformer turns ratio for a half-bridge LLC that converts a 400 V DC bus to 48 V near unity gain.

\[ n \approx \frac{V_{in}}{2V_o} = \frac{400}{2\cdot48} = 4.17 \]

Takeaway. A practical first-pass ratio is near 4:1. The exact selection still depends on rectifier drops, control range, magnetics, and allowed gain at low-line operation.
