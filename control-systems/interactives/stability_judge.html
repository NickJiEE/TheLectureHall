Call it: stable or unstable?

A pole-zero plot of a system is generated on the s-plane each round. Decide whether the system they define is stable or unstable.

s-planePole–zero plotStability

The one rule

A continuous-time linear system is stable exactly when every pole of its transfer function sits in the open left half of the s-plane — that is, when each pole has a strictly negative real part:

\[ \text{stable} \iff \operatorname{Re}(p_i) < 0 \quad \text{for every pole } p_i \]

Poles are drawn as ×, zeros as ○. The horizontal axis is the real part \( \sigma \); the vertical axis is the imaginary part \( j\omega \). Complex poles always arrive as conjugate pairs, mirrored above and below the real axis at \( \sigma \pm j\omega \). Only the poles decide stability — zeros shape the response, but a zero in the right half-plane never makes a system unstable. A pole sitting exactly on the imaginary axis (\( \sigma = 0 \)) is marginal, and we count that as unstable here.

How to read the plot

Sweep your eye from left to right and stop at the imaginary axis. Everything in the shaded right half is the danger zone.

  • Ignore the zeros. A zero (○) can sit anywhere — far right, on the axis, doesn't matter. It bends the shape of the response but never destabilises it. Train yourself to look past them.
  • Scan the poles (×). If every × is strictly to the left of the vertical axis, the system is stable. The moment one × lands on the axis or to its right, it's unstable.
  • Conjugate pairs move together. A complex pole and its mirror image share the same horizontal position, so they cross into the right half-plane at the same instant. Checking the top one tells you about the bottom one.
  • The axis itself counts as unstable. A pole at \( \sigma = 0 \) gives a response that neither grows nor decays — undamped oscillation or a pure integrator. That's marginal, not stable.
After you answer, any pole that breaks the rule is ringed in red so you can see exactly what tipped the system over. The factored transfer function under the plot shows the same poles and zeros algebraically — the denominator's roots are the poles you're judging.
