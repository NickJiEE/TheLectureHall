Control Systems

Tune the loop until the response behaves.

A PID controller watches the error between where you asked the system to go and where it actually is, then drives it with a weighted blend of that error, its accumulated history, and its rate of change. The plant below is a canonical second-order system — a mass–spring–damper, a motor position loop, an RLC circuit. Move the sliders and watch the unit-step response form in real time.

The model

The plant is the standard second-order form, with natural frequency \( \omega_n \) and damping ratio \( \zeta \):

\[ \ddot{y} + 2\zeta\omega_n\,\dot{y} + \omega_n^2\,y = \omega_n^2\,u \]

Choosing the state variables \( x_1 = y \) and \( x_2 = \dot{y} \) turns that single second-order equation into two first-order equations the integrator can march forward:

\[ \dot{x}_1 = x_2, \qquad \dot{x}_2 = -2\zeta\omega_n\,x_2 - \omega_n^2\,x_1 + \omega_n^2\,u, \qquad y = x_1 \]

The reference is a unit step, \( r = 1 \). The controller acts on the error \( e = r - y \) with three terms — proportional, integral, and derivative:

\[ u(t) = K_p\,e \;+\; K_i\!\int_0^{t} e\,d\tau \;+\; K_d\,\frac{de}{dt} \]

How to read it

Proportional \( K_p \) pushes harder the further you are from target — it speeds up the response but, on its own, leaves a steady-state error and eventually causes ringing. Integral \( K_i \) accumulates past error, so it erases that steady-state gap, but too much of it winds up and provokes overshoot. Derivative \( K_d \) reacts to how fast the error is closing, adding damping that tames overshoot and oscillation at the cost of sensitivity to noise.

  • Sluggish (P-only): small \( K_p \), no \( K_i \) — the output crawls toward the setpoint and stops short. The steady-state error is exactly \( 1/(1+K_p) \).
  • Underdamped: large \( K_p \) with little damping — a fast but bouncy response with large overshoot and a long settling time.
  • Tuned PID: a balanced set of gains — fast rise, small overshoot, zero steady-state error.
  • Unstable: push \( K_i \) high against a fast, lightly damped plant and the poles cross into the right half-plane — the response flies off the top of the chart and the status flips to Unstable.
There is no single "best" tuning. You determine the trade-offs that best fit your application the most regarding rise time, overshoot, settling time, control effort, or noise rejection. The sliders let you feel where one improves only by sacrificing another.
