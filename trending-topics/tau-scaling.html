Post-Moore Semiconductor Scaling

When shrinking stops working, compress time.

For six decades, Moore's Law drove progress by making transistors smaller. But geometric scaling has stalled: gate lengths have barely moved from 20 nm to 16 nm across a decade, while wafer costs have climbed by orders of magnitude. Huawei's τ (tau) Scaling Law proposes a new primary metric — not the transistor's physical size, but the characteristic time constant τ that determines how fast signals propagate through an entire computing stack, from device to data center.

Intuition

Moore's Law is fundamentally about geometry: halve the transistor area, get twice the transistors. The scaling metric is length L (in nm). What replaces it when lithography can no longer shrink L reliably?

Huawei's answer: optimise τ — a single characteristic time constant that unifies delay contributions across all abstraction levels of a computing system, from transistor switching speed (picoseconds) up to system-level communication latency (milliseconds). The central claim is that compressing τ at every level simultaneously — through 3D stacking, hybrid bonding, near-memory architecture, and hardware-software co-optimization — delivers the same exponential performance and density improvements Moore's Law once provided through geometry alone.

\[ \tau = f\!\left(\tau_{tr},\; \tau_{circuit},\; \tau_{chip},\; \tau_{sys}\right) \]

Each level has its own objective function and temporal scale, from the picosecond domain at the transistor level up through circuits, chips, and full data-center systems at the millisecond-to-second scale.

Design intuition: Moore's Law asked "how small can the transistor be?" τ Scaling asks "how short can the signal path be — in time, not just in space?" The two metrics often align, but not always. A 3D-stacked chip with older-node silicon can beat a 2D cutting-edge chip if the vertical interconnect dramatically shortens the critical timing path.

The τ hierarchy

The framework decomposes τ into four coupled levels. Each has a distinct objective function and operates over a different temporal range:

Device
\(\tau_{device} = (R_{gate}+R_{net})\cdot(C_{gate}+C_{load})\)
RC delay of a single gate — FEOL / BEOL optimization
ps
Circuit
\(\tau_{circuit} = f\!\bigl(\Sigma(\tau_{transistor} + \tau_{interconnect}),\;DTCO\bigr)\)
Critical path delay — layout, routing, DTCO
ns
Chip
\(\tau_{chip} = f\!\bigl(\Sigma\tau_{cir_{XPU}},\;\Sigma\tau_{cir_{MBM}},\;\Sigma\tau_{cir_{IO}},\;N_{parall},\;\eta_{eff},\;M_P\bigr)\)
XPU compute + memory bandwidth + IO latency
μs
System
\(\tau_{sys} = f\!\bigl(\Sigma\tau_{chip},\;\Sigma\tau_{comm},\;N_{parall},\;\eta_{eff},\;M_P,\;RAS,\;STCO\bigr)\)
Multi-chip, rack, pod, data-center
ms – s

The key insight is that each level can be separately improved and that gains are multiplicative across levels. Cutting τ at the device level while simultaneously reducing τ at the chip level (through 3D stacking that shortens memory-to-compute distances) and the system level (through SW-HW co-optimisation) compounds into performance gains that no single-level approach can match.

LogicFolding: the key enabling technology

LogicFolding is Huawei's production 3D IC methodology. Rather than placing all logic in a single 2D plane, it partitions the chip vertically — stacking multiple active silicon tiers bonded face-to-face using hybrid bonding. The fundamental win is geometric: connections that would otherwise run millimetres across a 2D die can instead travel a few microns vertically through the bond interface.

Why hybrid bonding enables this

Classical chiplet integration uses microbumps with pitches of 50–100 µm. Hybrid bonding (Cu–Cu direct bonding) achieves connection pitches below 2 µm and bond temperatures below 300 °C, making the interconnect density between dies nearly comparable to on-die routing. Each 100 mm² of active area can contain tens of millions of hybrid-bond connections and hundreds of thousands of TSVs, creating a true monolithic-like 3D interconnect fabric.

The SRAM improvements come from converting horizontal critical paths into vertical ones. Moving data decoder routing to the top metal layer and physically stacking SRAM over logic cuts the address-to-data RC path dramatically — the very definition of τ reduction.

τ-Scaling roadmap

Huawei's published roadmap projects circuit density and P-core clock frequency through 2031 assuming continued LogicFolding improvements. The trajectory is notably steeper than any available lithography-only path, particularly from 2026 onward when the Kirin 2026 (238 MTr/mm², 3.1 GHz) validates the first production generation of the approach.

System folding: the 2.5D dilemma

A conventional 2.5D design places HBM dies around a central logic die on an interposer. The logic die must then be large enough to reach all the memory — creating an N² scaling problem where die size, performance, and power all grow together. System folding inverts this by stacking logic, DRAM, and NAND layers vertically with TSV-based interconnects, replacing the flat N² footprint with a compact N-bandwidth-per-layer structure. Huawei's roadmap projects this approach delivers 4× hardware integration improvement by 2028 and more than 100× by 2035.

Thermal: the hard constraint

3D stacking concentrates power density. Thermal management must therefore span the same hierarchy as τ itself: transient thermal shock at the device level (ps–ns), local hot-spots at the circuit level (ns–µs), vertical conduction at the chip level, VRM miniaturisation at the board level, and PUE requirements at the data-center level.

Folding-oriented methodology explicitly includes thermal paths as a concurrent optimization objective alongside wirelength and timing. The SRAM folding results illustrate this: the move from horizontal to vertical critical paths also improves local thermal conduction because the thermal path to the package is now in the same direction as the signal path.

Key design tension: hybrid-bonding process temperatures must stay below 300 °C to avoid damaging the lower tier's existing BEOL stack. This constrains which dielectric and barrier materials can be used and is an active research frontier.

Worked example: where does τ go?

Problem. A 2D CPU core has a critical path with 40 logic stages. The path crosses the chip twice, totalling ~4 mm of interconnect at metal layers M3–M5. The interconnect RC contribution is 60% of total cycle time at 3 GHz. Estimate the τcircuit reduction from LogicFolding that halves the interconnect length.

RC model. For a wire of resistance R and capacitance C, the delay scales as \(\tau \sim \frac{1}{2}RC \propto L^2\) for a distributed RC line. Halving the wire length therefore reduces interconnect delay by a factor of 4 (since both R and C scale with L).

\[ \tau_{interconnect} \propto R \cdot C \propto L^2 \]

Idealized result. Interconnect accounts for 60% of the 333 ps cycle period, or approximately 200 ps. The remaining logic, clocking, and other fixed delay is approximately 133 ps. If every relevant wire segment were truly halved, the distributed-wire estimate would reduce the interconnect portion to 50 ps, giving a new critical-path delay of approximately 183 ps and an idealized upper-bound frequency of approximately 5.46 GHz.

Why measured gains are smaller. That upper bound is not directly comparable to a production frequency result. Only selected routes can be folded; vias and bond interfaces add parasitics; some delay does not scale with wire length; and power, thermal, clock-distribution, and timing-closure limits remain. A measured 12.7% clock increase can therefore be entirely plausible even though the simplified RC-only calculation predicts a much larger theoretical gain.

Takeaway. You do not need a new fab node to reduce τ. Re-routing selected critical paths vertically through a hybrid-bond interface can shorten wire length and reduce the distributed RC delay, which scales approximately with L² in this simplified model.

Questions worth thinking about

τ Scaling is a compelling framework, but it raises several design and methodology questions that don't yet have settled answers:

Equivalent node claims. Huawei has referred to a future LogicFolding target described as equivalent to a 1.4 nm process. From a design perspective, "equivalent" is not the same as lithographic node — it is a density and performance claim. What metrics (MTr/mm², frequency, power efficiency) would make such an equivalence verifiable?

EDA tooling gap. Pseudo-3D flows (module-level tier assignment after synthesis) are supported by today's commercial EDA tools. True-3D flows — where cells inside the same logic module can migrate across tier boundaries during placement — require fundamentally new placement engines, 3D static timing analysis, and cross-tier DRC. The gap between what is claimed and what EDA infrastructure can deliver is real and active.

Thermal vs. density tradeoff. Every additional folded tier improves density and latency but worsens thermal resistance to the package. The optimum number of tiers is a function of power density, cooling system, and workload — not a fixed number.
